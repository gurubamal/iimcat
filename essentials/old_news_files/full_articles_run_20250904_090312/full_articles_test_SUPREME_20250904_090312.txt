Trump Administration Rushes Tariff Case to Supreme Court
Donald Trump’s administration has asked the U.S. Supreme Court to urgently hear its appeal to preserve sweeping tariffs imposed under a 1977 emergency law. A lower court recently struck down most of these levies, dealing a blow to Trump’s trade agenda. (Source: Reuters)
AP
2/7
Justice Department Appeal
The DOJ appealed an August 29 ruling that said Trump exceeded his authority under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). The administration requested a fast-track review, with a decision by Sept. 10 and hearings in November.
ETMarkets.com
3/7
Administration’s Argument
Solicitor General D. John Sauer argued the tariffs are vital for peace, prosperity, and defense against trade retaliation. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent added that the ruling weakens Trump’s ability to conduct diplomacy and safeguard national security.
ETMarkets.com
4/7
Challenges to Tariffs
Small businesses and 12 Democratic-led states have challenged the tariffs, saying they violate the Constitution, which gives Congress—not the president—the power to levy tariffs. Critics argue IEEPA was never intended for such broad trade measures.
TIMESOFINDIA.COM
5/7
Court Rulings Against Trump
The Federal Circuit Court ruled 7-4 that IEEPA does not authorize tariffs, citing the “major questions” doctrine that requires clear congressional approval for actions of vast economic and political significance. Multiple courts have sided against Trump.
NYT News Service
6/7
Impact of Tariffs
Since returning to office, Trump has made tariffs central to U.S. foreign policy—using them to pressure countries and renegotiate deals. But they have triggered trade tensions, financial market volatility, and global economic uncertainty.

What’s Next
The Supreme Court’s decision on whether to take up the case could reshape the limits of presidential power on trade. The legal battle also coincides with challenges to Trump’s control over the Federal Reserve, setting up a broader clash over his economic policies.
